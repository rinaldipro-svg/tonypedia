In the basement of a nondescript office building in Astana, Kazakhstan, a digital dashboard tracks something few people outside the energy world think about: the real-time flow of uranium from the Central Asian steppe to reactor halls scattered across four continents. Kazakhstan's state-owned Kazatomprom pumps roughly four out of every ten pounds of uranium the world consumes, most of it dissolved from sandstone aquifers and piped to the surface through a technique called in-situ leaching. The process is quiet, invisible from the road, and staggeringly consequential. Without it, dozens of nuclear reactors from South Korea to Sweden would face fuel shortages within months.

Uranium has re-entered the global spotlight with a force that would have seemed improbable a decade ago. After years of post-Fukushima malaise, the nuclear industry is experiencing its most significant expansion since the 1980s. The drivers are familiar in outline — climate targets, energy security, baseload power — but the scale is new. The World Nuclear Association's latest reference scenario projects installed nuclear capacity nearly doubling from 398 GWe to 746 GWe by 2040. Reactor fuel requirements, currently around 69,000 metric tons of uranium per year, could surpass 150,000 metric tons by the same date. In a high-growth scenario, that figure climbs above 204,000 metric tons. The uranium market, long a sleepy corner of commodities trading, is being reshaped by forces that extend well beyond the mine gate.

## The Demand Surge No One Saw Coming

Three converging forces are redrawing the demand curve for uranium, each reinforcing the others in ways that make the trend difficult to reverse.

The first is the sheer momentum of reactor construction in Asia. China now operates 58 nuclear reactors with a combined capacity of roughly 56 GWe, and has 33 more units under construction. In April 2025, the State Council approved ten additional reactors — eight Hualong One units and two CAP1000s — representing an investment of roughly 200 billion yuan. By mid-2026, China is expected to surpass France as the world's second-largest nuclear power by installed capacity, a milestone that seemed decades away as recently as 2020. India, though moving more slowly, had seven reactors under construction as of mid-2024, with plans to accelerate its build-out as part of a broader clean energy strategy. South Korea, after a brief flirtation with a nuclear phase-out under the Moon Jae-in administration, has reversed course and is extending the lives of existing reactors while pursuing new exports.

The second force is the Western nuclear revival, driven partly by decarbonization commitments and partly by the discovery that intermittent renewables alone cannot keep data centers and industrial grids humming around the clock. The United States government has committed roughly $80 billion to a new fleet of AP1000 reactors built by Westinghouse. Goldman Sachs projects that America will build 20 new reactors and restart three mothballed units between now and 2045, adding an estimated 12.5 million pounds of annual uranium demand once those plants reach full operation. France, after years of ambivalence, has recommitted to nuclear under President Macron. The United Kingdom, Sweden, and several Eastern European nations have all announced new-build programs or lifetime extensions.

The third — and perhaps most unexpected — driver is artificial intelligence. The electricity demands of large language models and the data centers that house them have created an almost insatiable appetite for reliable baseload power. Tech companies including Microsoft, Google, and Amazon have all explored or signed agreements for nuclear-powered data center capacity. The narrative shift has been rapid: as recently as 2023, nuclear barely featured in conversations about AI infrastructure. By 2025, it was central to them. BMO Capital Markets notes that the World Nuclear Association has revised its uranium demand growth forecast upward to a 5.3 percent compound annual growth rate through 2040, from 4.1 percent previously — a substantial recalibration that reflects these new demand sources.

## A Supply Chain Under Strain

If demand is surging, supply is struggling to keep pace — and the reasons are structural rather than cyclical.

In 2024, global uranium mine production met only about 90 percent of reactor requirements. The remaining shortfall was covered by drawdowns from commercial and government stockpiles, a pattern that has persisted, with interruptions, for years. Total mine output came in around 60,600 metric tons, with modest growth of roughly 2.6 percent expected in 2025, bringing production to an estimated 62,200 metric tons. These figures sound respectable until you compare them with the demand trajectory. Goldman Sachs' updated supply-demand model calculates that the cumulative net deficit between 2025 and 2045 has widened by 211 million pounds compared with earlier estimates.

The bottleneck is not geological — the earth contains plenty of uranium. It is economic and operational. Mines that were shuttered during the long price downturn of 2011 to 2020 cannot be restarted overnight. Cameco's McArthur River mine in Saskatchewan, one of the world's highest-grade deposits, lowered its 2025 production guidance due to development delays. In Niger, the SOMAÏR mine produced nothing in 2025 after the military junta that seized power in 2023 disrupted operations. Several in-situ recovery projects in the American West have ramped up more slowly than their developers promised. Even Kazatomprom, the dominant global producer, signaled a reduction in its nominal 2026 production level, citing a cautious approach to capacity expansion.

Industry analysts forecast a modest production compound annual growth rate of around 4.1 percent through 2030, which would bring global output to approximately 76,800 metric tons. But beyond 2030, many existing mines are expected to plateau or decline without significant new project development — projects that typically require a decade or more from discovery to first production.

## Where the Uranium Sits — And Who Digs It Up

The geography of uranium reserves is strikingly concentrated. Australia holds nearly 30 percent of the world's identified uranium resources — roughly 1.7 million metric tons at the lowest extraction cost tier — yet produces almost none of it, constrained by state-level mining bans and political ambivalence toward nuclear energy. Kazakhstan follows with reserves likely exceeding 900,000 metric tons. Canada holds about 588,500 metric tons, or roughly 10 percent of global resources. Russia and Namibia each account for about 8 percent, at approximately 470,000 metric tons apiece. In total, fewer than ten countries control over 90 percent of all identified reserves, making uranium one of the most geographically concentrated strategic minerals on the planet.

Production tells a different story from reserves. Kazakhstan dominated in 2024, accounting for 39 percent of global mine output. Canada contributed 24 percent, largely from the extraordinarily rich deposits of the Athabasca Basin in northern Saskatchewan. Namibia, benefiting from the ramp-up of the Langer Heinrich mine, provided about 12 percent. Uzbekistan, Australia, Russia, and Niger filled out most of the remainder. The concentration of production in a handful of jurisdictions — several of which are geopolitically complex — is a source of growing anxiety for Western utilities.

The corporate landscape is equally top-heavy. Kazatomprom, a state-controlled enterprise listed in both London and Astana, is the single largest uranium producer on earth. Cameco, a publicly traded Canadian company, ranks second, holding majority stakes in Cigar Lake (the world's most productive uranium mine at 54.55 percent) and the McArthur River-Key Lake complex. France's Orano (formerly Areva) operates mines in Canada and Kazakhstan and remains a major player in conversion and enrichment. Beyond these three, the field fragments into a constellation of junior miners, development-stage companies, and state enterprises — many of which are listed on the Toronto or Australian stock exchanges and are betting on a sustained price upcycle to justify their exploration spending.

## The Enrichment Chokepoint

Mining uranium is only the first step in a long and technically demanding fuel cycle. Before yellowcake can power a reactor, it must be converted to uranium hexafluoride, enriched to increase the concentration of the fissile isotope U-235, and fabricated into fuel assemblies. Each stage presents its own set of geopolitical risks.

Russia's Rosatom controls approximately 44 percent of global uranium enrichment capacity, along with about 17 percent of fuel fabrication. For decades, Western utilities relied on Russian enrichment services as a matter of economic convenience — Rosatom's prices were competitive, its technology proven. The invasion of Ukraine in 2022 shattered that calculus. European and American utilities scrambled to diversify their supply chains, but enrichment capacity cannot be spun up quickly. Centrifuge cascades are precision-engineered, capital-intensive, and subject to strict nonproliferation controls.

URENCO, the European enrichment consortium with plants in Germany, the Netherlands, the United Kingdom, and New Mexico, has been expanding to fill part of the gap. Its American facility brought a new centrifuge cascade online in May 2025, with a second following ahead of schedule in September. Orano operates enrichment facilities in France. China National Nuclear Corporation has been building out domestic enrichment capacity to reduce its dependence on foreign suppliers. Together, Rosatom and CNNC controlled more than 62 percent of global separative work unit (SWU) capacity in 2024, a concentration that underscores the West's vulnerability in this critical segment of the nuclear fuel chain.

The enrichment market is projected to grow from roughly $14.2 billion in 2025 to $22.2 billion by 2030, a compound annual growth rate of about 9.3 percent. Much of that growth will be driven by the need to replace Russian services and to fuel the next generation of advanced reactors, some of which require high-assay low-enriched uranium (HALEU) that is currently produced almost exclusively in Russia.

## The Nuclear Technology Map

The countries that matter most in nuclear technology do not always overlap with those that mine or enrich uranium. The United States, despite its aging reactor fleet, remains the world's largest producer of nuclear electricity and the home of Westinghouse, whose AP1000 design is being built in China, the U.K., Poland, and potentially several other markets. France, through EDF and its supply chain, operates the most nuclear-dependent grid on earth (roughly 70 percent of electricity) and is developing the EPR2 as its next-generation design. Russia's Rosatom has built or is building reactors in Turkey, Egypt, Bangladesh, India, and Hungary, and offers a vertically integrated package — financing, construction, fuel supply, and waste management — that few competitors can match.

China is emerging as the most dynamic player. Beyond the sheer volume of its domestic construction, Beijing is pushing frontiers in reactor technology. In January 2026, China broke ground on a first-of-its-kind hybrid project pairing conventional pressurized water reactors with an advanced high-temperature gas-cooled reactor, a fourth-generation design that promises higher efficiency and the ability to produce industrial process heat. The Hualong One, China's indigenous third-generation design, is being marketed for export and has already been built in Pakistan.

South Korea's KHNP is competing aggressively for export contracts, buoyed by the strong operational track record of its APR-1400 design at the Barakah plant in the United Arab Emirates. Canada's CANDU reactor technology, while no longer at the forefront of new builds, remains relevant through lifetime extensions and the development of small modular reactors by companies like Terrestrial Energy. Small modular reactors — factory-built units with capacities typically below 300 MWe — are attracting attention from the United States, Canada, the U.K., and several emerging nuclear nations, though none has yet reached commercial operation at scale.

## Prices, Contracts, and the Question of Incentive

For all the bullish talk, the uranium spot price spent most of 2025 in a holding pattern, oscillating between $63 and $83 per pound of U3O8. The annual average came in at $73.54 per pound — a level that is historically elevated but well below what many analysts believe is necessary to incentivize the construction of new mines. Long-term contract prices fared better, reaching a 14-year high of $86.50 per pound in December 2025. Approximately 116 million pounds of uranium were placed under long-term contracts by utilities during the year, though this volume remained below the replacement rate, potentially increasing the backlog of uncovered future requirements.

Goldman Sachs projects spot prices reaching $91 per pound by the end of 2026, while some independent forecasters see prices exceeding $110 if contracting activity accelerates and supply disruptions persist. The fundamental tension is straightforward: producers want sustained higher prices before committing capital to new projects, while utilities, conditioned by years of cheap uranium, have been slow to lock in long-term supply at today's rates. The result is a kind of stalemate that virtually guarantees tighter markets ahead.

The reinstatement of uranium on the U.S. Geological Survey's 2025 list of critical minerals — a designation it had previously lost — signals that Washington now views fuel supply through a national security lens. Sanctions on Russian nuclear fuel services, combined with export restrictions and the broader fragmentation of global trade, are compressing the number of jurisdictions from which Western utilities can reliably source material. The era of treating uranium as a commodity of convenience, procured from whichever seller offered the lowest price, appears to be ending.

## What Comes Next

The uranium industry in 2026 sits at an inflection point that is less about any single variable than about the convergence of several. Demand is growing faster than at any time since the first oil crisis of the 1970s, driven by a coalition of climate policy, energy security, and the ravenous electricity needs of artificial intelligence. Supply remains constrained by years of underinvestment, operational setbacks, and the inherent difficulty of bringing new mines into production. The enrichment segment — long Russia's quiet monopoly — is being restructured under geopolitical pressure, but the transition will take years and billions of dollars.

For mining-dependent nations like Kazakhstan, Canada, and Namibia, the boom presents both opportunity and risk: higher revenues, but also the challenge of managing resource nationalism, environmental scrutiny, and the expectations of a global market that needs them more than ever. For technology leaders like the United States, France, China, and Russia, the contest is about more than reactor exports — it is about controlling the strategic infrastructure of twenty-first-century energy. And for the dozens of countries that operate or plan to build nuclear reactors, the next decade will test whether the uranium supply chain can scale fast enough to match ambitions that, for the first time in a generation, outrun the fuel available to realize them.
